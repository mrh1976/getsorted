The structure

The price is just
where it starts.

Every deal type has its own hidden variables. Here's where the money moves depending on how you're buying.

Lease
Four variables.
One you're shown.
A lease has four moving parts: cap cost, money factor, residual, and fees. Dealers can make the monthly look great by adjusting any one while quietly taking back ground on the others. Most people only ever discuss the payment.
Where the money goes

Money factor markups above the manufacturer base rate — worth $50–$100 per month you never had to pay.

Dealer add-ons pre-loaded into the cap cost. On a lease you pay interest on them for 36 months.

Acquisition fees that vary by dealer and are almost never disclosed unprompted.

Finance or Cash
Win at the desk.
Lose in the box.
You negotiated hard on price. You're tired. Now the finance manager presents a menu of products in a room designed for exactly that moment. The F&I office generates more profit per deal than the entire sales floor.
Where the money goes

Interest rate markups above the lender's buy rate — the dealer keeps the spread, just like a mortgage broker.

Loan term extension without disclosure — your payment drops $60, your total cost climbs $3,000.

Extended warranties priced at 2–3x their actual market value.

Trade-In
Two numbers.
You only know one.
Your trade has a real market value and a dealer offer. Those are almost always different numbers. The dealer controls when and how the trade enters the conversation — and uses it throughout.
Where the money goes

Inflated trade offer to build goodwill, quietly recovered through a higher purchase price.

Undervalued offers on cars the dealer knows they'll wholesale for significantly more within 48 hours.

Negative equity rolled into your new loan without clear disclosure of the total cost impact.
