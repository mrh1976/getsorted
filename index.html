Where the money goes

Here's what they know
that you don't.

A dealership has multiple profit centers in every single deal. Most buyers are aware of one. Here are the ones that cost you the most.

01
The Price Stack
The sticker is not the dealer's cost. Between MSRP and what the dealer actually paid sit invoice price, holdback, dealer cash, and regional incentives. These are layers of manufacturer-to-dealer money that never appear on any consumer document. On a $50,000 vehicle the gap between sticker and true dealer cost can exceed $6,000. That gap is entirely negotiable if you know it exists.
Typical savings opportunity
$1,500 – $6,000
02
The Lease Structure
A lease has four variables that determine what you actually pay: the selling price, the money factor, the residual value, and the fees. Dealers mark up the money factor above what the manufacturer sets and pocket the spread, exactly like a mortgage broker marking up a rate. Most buyers never know the base rate exists. A $30 per month markup over 36 months is $1,080 you never had to spend.
Typical savings opportunity
$800 – $3,600
03
The F&I Room
The finance and insurance office generates more profit per deal than the entire sales floor. You've just spent two hours negotiating the price. You're tired, relieved, and ready to be done. The finance manager knows this. Extended warranties at three times market value, GAP insurance you can buy cheaper elsewhere, paint protection that costs the dealer almost nothing. Every product is presented in a sequence designed to move you from no to yes.
Typical savings opportunity
$500 – $2,500
04
Your Trade-In
Your trade has a real market value and a dealer offer. Those are almost always different numbers, and the dealer controls when and how the trade enters the conversation. Introduce it too early and it becomes a variable they use to recover ground on the purchase price. They know what your car will bring at auction. You don't. The sequence of that conversation matters as much as the numbers.
Typical savings opportunity
$1,000 – $3,000
05
Dealer Add-Ons
Before you sit down, your vehicle may already have $500 to $2,000 in dealer-installed products added to the price. Paint sealant applied with a consumer-grade buffer. Nitrogen tire fills. VIN etching. Door edge guards. These are presented as mandatory. They are not. On a lease, you pay interest on every one of them for the entire term. Knowing they are negotiable and being willing to say so can be free money.
Typical savings opportunity
$500 – $2,000
06
The Financing Rate
When you finance through the dealership, the lender gives the dealer a buy rate, which is the lowest rate your credit qualifies for. The dealer is permitted to mark that rate up and keep the difference. You are almost never told the buy rate. On a $40,000 loan over 60 months, one percentage point costs you approximately $1,000. Knowing your rate before you walk in changes the entire conversation in the finance office.
Typical savings opportunity
$500 – $2,000
Total potential savings across all six areas
$4,800 – $19,100
Not every deal has room in every category. But every deal has room somewhere. We know where to look.
