The Sticker Behind The Sticker

Market Adjustment. Additional Dealer Markup. Whatever They Call It, Here's What It Actually Is.

July 24, 2026 5 min read

You found the car. You found the price online. You get to the dealership and there's a second sticker sitting right next to the window sticker, a market adjustment, sometimes called ADM, additional dealer markup, or a dealer-added value package. It adds anywhere from a few hundred dollars to several thousand on top of what Hyundai, Toyota, or Ford told you the car should cost.

Here's the part that surprises most people. It's legal. In nearly every state, a dealer is free to sell a car for whatever price a buyer agrees to pay, above MSRP included. The manufacturer sets a suggested price. The dealer sets the actual price. Those are two different documents, and only one of them is binding.

That said, it isn't unlimited. Some manufacturers have pushed back hard on dealers who lean on ADM too aggressively, restricting future vehicle allocation to dealers who mark up high-demand models, since it damages brand trust with the very customers the manufacturer worked to attract. And the FTC's CARS Rule specifically targets deceptive advertising, if a dealer advertises a price online that isn't actually available once you walk in, that's a different problem than a disclosed markup, and it's the kind of thing regulators have gone after directly.

Is it worth what they're charging?

The practical question isn't whether ADM is legal. It usually is. The question is what you're actually paying for. Sometimes a market adjustment reflects genuine scarcity, a hot model with real demand and thin inventory. Other times it's a flat add-on slapped on every unit on the lot regardless of demand, hoping buyers won't ask.

How to actually handle it

Ask the dealer directly whether the advertised or MSRP price is available without the adjustment. Some dealers will negotiate it down or remove it entirely once they realize you know it's a line item, not a law. Others hold firm, especially on genuinely scarce vehicles, in which case your real leverage is simply shopping a wider radius, a dealer two counties over without the same allocation pressure may not be charging it at all.

This is exactly the kind of line item that's easy to miss when you're negotiating your own deal and hard to catch once it's buried in a four square worksheet alongside everything else. It's also exactly the kind of thing we check line by line before you ever sit down at a table.
