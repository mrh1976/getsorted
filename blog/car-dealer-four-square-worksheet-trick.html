The Four Square Worksheet Is Designed to Confuse You. Here's Proof.

June 22, 2026 · 5 min read

If you've ever bought a car, there's a good chance someone slid a piece of paper across the desk to you that looked something like a tax form having a bad day. Four boxes. A lot of handwriting. Numbers crossed out and rewritten two or three times before anyone agreed on anything.

That's the four square worksheet, and it's one of the oldest tools in the car business. We got our hands on a real one from an actual deal, and looking at it tells you more about how dealerships negotiate than any explanation could.

What you're actually looking at

The four square worksheet splits a car deal into four boxes: the vehicle's sale price, your trade-in value, your down payment, and your monthly payment. On paper, that sounds organized. Four numbers, four boxes, easy to follow.

In practice, look at what happened on the worksheet above. The trade value got circled, crossed out, and rewritten. The cash down number changed twice. The monthly payment box has three different figures stacked on top of each other, each one circled at a different point in the conversation.

A worksheet this hard to follow isn't bad handwriting. It's a structure that makes it nearly impossible to track where one number moved to cover up movement somewhere else.

Why four boxes instead of one

Here's the part that matters. If a dealer only negotiated one number, the purchase price, you'd see exactly how much ground was given up and exactly how much you walked away with. Simple, transparent, done.

Four boxes changes that completely. Now there are four separate places a number can move, and every time one box shifts, it can be quietly offset somewhere else on the page. You push the price down. They adjust the trade value to compensate. You push the trade up. They stretch the loan term to make the payment work. By the time all four boxes have moved two or three times, almost nobody in the room, including the salesperson sometimes, can tell you exactly where the actual savings landed.

That's not an accident. It's the entire design of the worksheet.

What the additional accessories line is doing there

Look closely at the worksheet and you'll notice a section for "additional accessories." Running boards. Fog lights. Wheel locks. An alarm system. Rear bumper guard. None of these were asked for in most deals. They show up pre-filled, tucked into a corner of the form most buyers glance past because their attention is locked on the bigger numbers in the other three boxes.

This is the same principle as the dealer add-ons we've written about before. Small dollar amounts, buried in a place buyers aren't focused on, add up fast. On the worksheet we have, those accessories alone account for over a thousand dollars that never got discussed out loud.

How to actually use this if you're ever handed one

You don't need to refuse a four square worksheet if a dealer hands you one. You just need to control how it gets filled out.

  • Negotiate one box at a time, not all four together. Lock the purchase price first. Get a number you're comfortable with, in writing, before anything else enters the conversation.
  • Bring up your trade-in separately, and later. Once the purchase price is locked, then talk trade value as its own conversation. Don't let the two get blended.
  • Ignore the monthly payment box until the other three are settled. A payment number means nothing on its own. It only means something once you know the actual price, the actual trade value, and the actual term.
  • Ask what's in the additional accessories line, every time. If you don't remember asking for it, ask why it's there and whether it can be removed.

The takeaway

The four square worksheet isn't evil. It's just a tool, and like most tools in a dealership, it works in whoever's favor understands it better. Most buyers have never seen one before they're sitting across from someone who uses it daily.

Now you have. Next time someone slides one across the desk, you'll know exactly why it has four boxes instead of one, and exactly how to keep your eyes on the number that actually matters.
